# Rayan — Internal Alignment Playbook

**Date:** March 16, 2026
**Audience:** Haroun, Samee, Anujan, Omer (internal only — nothing here goes to Rayan)

---

## The Problem

Rayan tunnels. He dives deep into debugging without stepping back to check fundamentals — wrong model config for a full day, repeated across integrations and PAM core. The work requires oversight the team can't afford to give, and the quality isn't where it needs to be for the level of autonomy the role demands. This isn't an attitude problem. It's a judgment and process problem that hasn't improved with informal feedback.

## The Move

Transition Rayan from PAM core to a dedicated support engineering role with a 60-day performance plan. The plan is built. Anujan wrote it. The criteria, tracking, escalation framework, and milestones are solid.

What we need to align on before anything reaches Rayan:

## Decision 1: Framing

There are two versions of this conversation and we need to pick one.

**Option A — PIP energy.** Rayan needs to feel the stakes. If he doesn't understand this could end in termination, he won't take it seriously. Samee's position.

**Option B — Direct without fear.** Rayan has never been told plainly what the bar is. Give him clarity and real consequences, but don't lead with threat. If he walks out thinking "they're trying to fire me," he won't invest in the plan. He'll perform out of anxiety for 60 days and leave anyway. Haroun's position.

**The middle ground:** Direct and serious is not the same as threatening. Rayan needs to understand the stakes are real without feeling like the outcome is already decided. The language in the plan should be clear about consequences (see Decision 3), but the delivery conversation should lead with "here's what we need from you and why we think you can do it."

**Decide this before the meeting happens.**

## Decision 2: Who Owns This

Whoever delivers the plan also runs the check-ins and makes the call at day 60. These can't be split.

| Person | Role |
|--------|------|
| **Anujan** | Delivers 1:1. Runs all check-ins. Makes the day 60 call. He built the plan, has the day-to-day context, and has the relationship. |
| **Omer** | Co-signs the document. Attends the 30/60-day formal reviews for management authority. Does not deliver. Does not run check-ins. |
| **Samee** | Separate 1:1 with Rayan — a day after delivery, not in the same meeting. Shares his own support eng story as mentorship, not as part of the PIP conversation. |
| **Haroun** | Not in the room for delivery. Available for escalation if Anujan needs backing. |

**Key question for Anujan:** Are you willing to make the hard call at day 60 if Rayan doesn't hit the bar? If the answer is no, we have a different problem.

## Decision 3: What "Unsuccessful" Means

Decide now, not at day 60. The plan currently says "the manager will determine next steps." That's too vague — Rayan will either read it as "I'm getting fired" or "this is toothless," and both are bad.

Two options, matching the framing decision:

**Option A (matches PIP energy):**
> "If performance does not meet the expectations outlined in this plan by day 60, employment will be terminated."

Termination is the headline. It's the first thing Rayan absorbs. Clear, no ambiguity, high stakes from word one.

**Option B (matches direct without fear):**
> "If performance does not meet the expectations outlined in this plan by day 60, we'll have an honest conversation about whether this role is the right fit for you at Pam. That may mean separation."

Termination is still on the table — but it's framed as the natural conclusion if things don't work, not as a threat. The difference is whether Rayan spends 60 days trying to save his job or trying to prove he can do this.

Both versions provide the same legal documentation. The distinction is entirely about what Rayan hears on day 0.

## The Pitch

This is the thing that determines whether Rayan buys in or checks out. Whoever delivers needs to internalize this:

> "Support engineering isn't a stepping stone — it's the role. The company's biggest bottleneck right now isn't shipping features, it's that the product breaks faster than anyone can fix it and nobody has owned that problem with real engineering discipline. The person who builds the systems, tooling, and documentation that makes support load disappear is solving a harder and more impactful problem than most of what the core team ships. That's what this role is."

**Be honest about this.** The plan currently says "this could mean continuing to grow in this role or moving to an IC engineering position." Cut the second half. Replace with: "Strong performance here demonstrates the ownership and technical capability we value most, and opens a conversation about growth within the support engineering function." Don't promise a path back to product if that's not the intent.

If Rayan hears "this is the role, permanently" and doesn't want it, you might get a resignation instead of a 60-day effort. That's not necessarily a bad outcome, but walk in knowing it's on the table.

## The Delivery Sequence

1. **Before anything:** Anujan pulls baseline metrics from Linear. Ticket volume, escalation rate, trailing 30-day average. Walk in with the numbers, not a promise to pull them later.
2. **Day 0 — Anujan delivers 1:1 to Rayan.** Walks through the plan, the criteria, the timeline. Answers questions. Lets Rayan react honestly without an audience. Documents the meeting (date, what was discussed, that Rayan acknowledged the plan).
3. **Day 1 or 2 — Samee has a separate 1:1.** Shares his career story — boiler room support eng at Upside, built credibility through the unglamorous work, used it to move into product. This is mentorship, not a pile-on. It lands because it's separated from the plan delivery.
4. **Day 2 or 3 — Haroun has a separate 1:1.** Reinforces the same message from a different angle: at our stage, support engineering is the highest-leverage work in the company. Product work that ships features nobody needs is not more important than engineering the support burden out of existence. This isn't a talking point — Haroun genuinely believes it, and Rayan hearing it from the CTO independently of Samee's story makes it real. Two founders saying the same thing separately is hard to dismiss as corporate spin.
5. **Ongoing — Anujan runs check-ins** at weeks 2, 4, 6, 8 per the plan.
