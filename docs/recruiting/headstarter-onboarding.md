### Role 1: Senior Engineer — New Product (0→1)

This person takes over a working MVP from the CTO and makes it their own. They own the product end-to-end: prompt engineering, data modeling, full-stack features, customer conversations. It's a product-owner role that happens to require strong engineering.

#### Persona 1A: The Entrepreneurial Builder

Someone who has started something of their own — a startup, a side project, a product — and seen it through, regardless of outcome. Former founders, indie hackers, people who've shipped end-to-end and learned from it.

**Why this fits the role:** This person will run a product pod solo. They need to make product decisions, talk to customers, set priorities, and ship — all without someone telling them what to do. Someone who's built their own thing has these instincts already.

**Signals to look for:**
- Founded or co-founded a company (any stage, any outcome)
- Built and shipped a product independently — can demo it or explain what happened
- Can articulate what they learned from something that didn't work
- Gets energized by ambiguity, not paralyzed by it
- Sees Pam as a stepping stone to eventually starting their own thing (we want that)

#### Persona 1B: The Scrappy Startup Generalist

Someone from a small team (under 30 people) who wore many hats — wrote code, talked to customers, set up infrastructure, fixed the build pipeline. No narrow specialty because they never had the luxury of one.

**Why this fits the role:** The 0→1 role touches everything: prompts, data models, dashboards, integrations, analytics. There's no "backend team" to hand things off to. This person needs to be comfortable across the full stack and comfortable being the only engineer on the product.

**Signals to look for:**
- Worked at an early-stage startup (Seed to Series A)
- Can point to shipped work across the stack, not just one layer
- Comfortable with imperfect systems — improves them incrementally, doesn't demand a rewrite
- Has built multi-channel systems (SMS, email, voice) or CRM integrations
- Uses AI tools daily (Cursor, Claude, Copilot) — this is how we work

#### Persona 1C: The Metrics-Driven Product Engineer

Someone who thinks about product success scientifically — designs experiments, tracks outcomes, iterates based on data. Not just a feature factory; they measure whether what they built actually worked.

**Why this fits the role:** This product competes with Impel and Podium. We can't just ship features and hope — we need someone who knows how to prove we're winning. The CTO built the MVP; this person needs to make it a real, measurable business.

**Signals to look for:**
- Has a framework for measuring product success (not just "shipped it")
- Experience with A/B testing, cohort analysis, or product analytics
- Can explain a time they killed or pivoted a feature based on data
- Comfortable presenting metrics to leadership and customers
- Previous PM-adjacent or product-engineer hybrid roles

### Role 2: Senior Software Engineer — LLM Reliability

This person owns the quality and reliability of Pam's AI across millions of live calls. Not a feature role — a "make it bulletproof" role. They build observability, reduce hallucinations, catch problems before customers do, and mentor junior engineers on reliability practices.

#### Persona 2A: The Production-Hardened Engineer

Someone who's been on-call for systems that matter. They've debugged at 2am, dealt with cascading failures, and care deeply about reliability — not just shipping features. They think in terms of observability, fallback strategies, and "what could go wrong."

**Why this fits the role:** Pam handles millions of live phone calls. When the AI says the wrong thing, a real customer at a real dealership has a bad experience. This person needs to have been burned by production before and come out better for it.

**Signals to look for:**
- Has war stories from production incidents (and can explain what they changed after)
- Thinks about monitoring and alerting before shipping, not after
- Comfortable saying "we shouldn't ship this yet" when quality isn't there
- Previous SRE, platform, or reliability engineering experience
- Has been on-call and has scars to show for it

#### Persona 2B: The AI Systems Tinkerer

Someone who's gone deep on making LLMs behave in production — not just prototyping with them. They've built eval frameworks, tuned prompts at scale, implemented fallback chains, or built guardrails around model outputs.

**Why this fits the role:** This isn't a "use the OpenAI API" role. This person needs to debug why Pam said the wrong thing on a call, figure out if it's a prompt issue, a context problem, or a model limitation, and fix it systematically — not just patch the one case.

**Signals to look for:**
- Has built LLM evaluation frameworks or quality monitoring systems
- Understands prompt engineering deeply — context windows, structured outputs, chain-of-thought
- Has opinions on model selection trade-offs (speed vs. quality vs. cost)
- Experience with voice AI, conversational systems, or real-time AI is a strong plus
- Reads ML papers or follows AI research — stays ahead of the curve

#### Persona 2C: The Quality-Obsessed Debugger

Someone whose instinct when something breaks is to go deep — read the logs, reproduce the issue, trace it to root cause, then fix the pattern so it never happens again. Not someone who applies band-aids.

**Why this fits the role:** LLM behavior issues are subtle — the model might work 95% of the time and fail in edge cases that only surface under specific conditions. This person needs the patience and rigor to track down the 5% and the judgment to know which failures matter most.

**Signals to look for:**
- Strong systematic debugging skills — can walk through their process
- Has built internal tooling for debugging or observability
- Fixes patterns, not instances — when they find a bug, they ask "where else does this happen?"
- Customer empathy — understands that every bad AI response is a real person's bad experience
- Can mentor junior engineers on how to think about reliability

## What We Screen For (Beyond Skills)

We hire in this order: **Culture → Aptitude → Skills.** TypeScript is non-negotiable (our entire stack), but everything else is learnable. Here's what actually matters:

| Signal | What We're Looking For |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Grit** | Wants the grind. Hears "this is the hardest job you'll ever have" and leans in. |
| **Ownership** | Owns outcomes, not tasks. Doesn't wait to be told what to do. |
| **Speed** | Ships fast. Code in production Day 1. Bias to action over analysis. |
| **AI-native** | Uses AI to code daily. Has opinions on prompt engineering. |
| **Ambition** | Wants to be a founder someday. Sees Pam as a launchpad, not a landing pad. |
| **Customer empathy** | Willing to get on calls, understand pain, translate it to product. |

**Dealbreakers:**
- "I need to understand the full architecture before I can contribute"
- Looking for work-life balance as priority #1 (we're not the right fit for that)
- Can't explain things simply
- Needs constant direction

## About Pam

Pam is the fastest-growing AI customer experience platform for car dealerships. We went from **0 to $10M ARR in 2 years**. 500+ dealerships, millions of calls handled, $100M+ in repair orders booked. Backed by Autotech Ventures, partnered with Tekion, CDK, Dealer-FX.

**Why engineers join Pam:**

- **You own a product, not a backlog.** We run product pods — small teams where the engineer is the product owner. You make decisions, talk to customers, and ship. The two open roles are both pod-lead positions: one owns a brand-new product line (0→1), the other owns LLM reliability across the platform.

- **It's an AI playground.** Our entire product is LLM-powered voice AI. You'll work on prompt engineering, model selection, context engineering, real-time conversation systems — the stuff most companies only talk about in blog posts.

- **You'll learn how to be a founding engineer.** Pam operates like an early-stage company — fast, scrappy, high-ownership. If your goal is to start your own company someday, this is the best training ground. You'll learn the same engineering principles that took us from 0 to $10M — and help us run them again to $50M+.

- **Ship Day 1.** No 90-day onboarding. No shadowing. You push code to production your first week. We trust you to figure it out — and we'll help when you're actually stuck.

**The team:** ~60 people, engineering-heavy. Distributed across US, Canada, LATAM, Pakistan, and the Middle East, with a strong in-office presence in Tysons Corner, VA. We strongly prefer in-person — the best collaboration happens in the room.

**Comp:** $160K–$200K base + equity in a company that 5x'd in two years and is just getting started.
